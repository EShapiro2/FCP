Unit 4: Enzymatic Reactions
Catalysis
A catalyst is a substance that increases the rate of a chemical reaction without itself being consumed
The catalyst may react to form an intermediate but it is regenerated in a subsequent step of the reaction
Catalysis
A catalyst speeds up a reaction by providing a set of elementary steps with more favorable kinetics than those that exist in its absence
The rate constant largely depends on the activation energy of a reaction (the minimum amount of energy required to initiate reaction)
Catalysis
In many cases, a catalyst increases the reaction rate by lowering the activation energy: In lowers it both for the forward and the reverse reaction
Enzyme Catalysis
Enzymes are biological catalysts
They are typically large protein molecules
They differ from chemical catalysts in four important aspects
Enzyme Catalysis
Higher reaction rates: enzymes increase the rates of biochemical reactions by 106-1012
Milder reaction conditions: Enzymatically catalyzed reactions occur at mild temperature, pressure, and nearly neutral pH.
Greater reaction specificity: Enzymes are typically highly specific, acting only on certain reactant molecules, called substrates, and rarely have side products
Capacity for regulation: The catalytic activities of many enzymes are affected by substances other than their substrates, by various mechanisms.
Enzyme Nomenclature
Alcohol dehydrogenase
Substrate Specificity
A variety of non-covalent bonds are involved in the binding of substrates to enzymes
The “Lock-and-Key Model:
A complementarity exists between enzymes and their substrates
Substrate Specificity
Geometric complementarity: A substrate-binding site on the enzyme consists of an indentation or cleft that is complementary in shape to the substrate
Electronic complementarity: The amino acids at the binding site are arranged to interact specifically with the substrate in an “attractive” manner
Induced-fit: The substrate-binding sites of most enzymes are largely preformed but undergo some conformational change on substrate binding

Co-factors
In some reactions, enzymes are associated with small molecule co-factors, which act as the enzyme’s “chemical teeth”.
There are two types of co-factors
Metal ions (e.g. Cu+2, Fe+3)
Organic molecules = co-enzymes (e.g. NAD+)
Co-enzymes
Co-enzymes are also distinguished by their type of association
Co-substrates are only transiently associated with a given enzyme molecule (e.g. NAD+)
Prosthetic groups are permanently associated with the protein, often by covalent bonds (e.g. heme group of cytochrome c)
Co-enzymes
Co-enzymes are chemically changed by the enzymatic reaction in which they participate
In order to complete the catalytic cycle, the coenzyme must be regenerated to its original state
For prosthetic groups regeneration occurs in a separate phase of the enzymatic reaction sequence
For co-substrates the regeneration reaction may be catalyzed by a different enzyme

Regulation of Enzymes
Many substances alter the activity of an enzyme by reversibly combining with it in the way that influences the binding of substrate or its turnover to product
Sunstances that reduce an enzyme’s activity in this way are termed reversible inhibitors
Enzyme Inhibitors
Enzyme inhibitors act through a variety of mechanisms, for example
Structurally resemble the substrate but either do not react or react very slowly
Affect catalytic activity without interfering with substrate binding.
Competitive Inhibition
Competitive inhibitor: A substance that competes directly with a normal substrate for an enyzme’s substrate-binding site
Usually resembles the substrate so that it can specifically bind to the active site…
But differs from the substrate so that it cannot react as the substrate does

An uncompetitive inhibitor binds directly to the enzyme-substrate complex but not to the free enzyme
It need not resemble the substrate, and presumably distorts the active site, thereby rendering the enzyme catalytically inactive
