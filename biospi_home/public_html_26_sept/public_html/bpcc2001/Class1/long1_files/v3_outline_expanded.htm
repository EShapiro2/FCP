The Chemical Bond
Chemical bonds as the “glue” that holds atoms together to form molecules
Theories of valence
Lewis electron-dot diagrams
VSEPR (valence shell electron-pair repulsion theory)
Both theories useful to correctly predict many features of bonding (but it must actually be explained by quantum mechanics)

Valence
The property of an atom to form bonds, e.g.
Univalent – capable of forming a single chemical bond
The valence may be unsigned or signed
Examples:
H – valence of 1
C – valence of 4
Na – valence of +1
Some elements (e.g. N, S, P) have multiple possible valences

Lewis’ Octet Theory
A covalent bond results when a pair of electrons is shared between two atoms (unsigned valence)
In ion formation, the sharing is so unequal that electrons are transferred from one atom to another (signed valence)

Lewis’ Octet Theory

Lewis’ Octet Theory
The first 2, 10, 18 (etc) electrons form a kernel or closed shell
Those electrons outside a kernel but not yet sufficient in number to form a full octet are the valence electrons, e.g.
Cl: 17 electrons – 10 kernel electrons  =
7 valence electrons
Sharing takes place so that both atoms can have filled octets

Octet Structures

Covalent Bond Diagrams
A bond is a sharing of a pair of electrons, so that both atoms have filled octets

Lone Electron Pairs

Double and Triple Bonds
Sharing of two or three pairs of electrons

Some Limitations
Quadruple bonds cannot be formed by most elements (including the first 20). Why?
O2 does not exactly follow the octet rule for binding
Resonance structures

Ionic Bonds
Filled octets achieved by gaining or losing electrons (rather than sharing)
As a result, each atom has a net positive or negative charge and is an ion
The attraction between positive and negative ions results in an ionic bond

Polar Bonds

Radicals
“Parts” of chemical formula of special significance, e.g.
H2O: H and OH radicals
CH3
Have an odd number of electrons
The ordinary free radical is univalent, and highly reactive

Bond and Bond-Dissociation Energies
In a diatomic molecule, the bond energy is the amount of energy required to convert a molecule into atoms.
In a polyatomic molecule the energy of atomization is the sum of all bond energies
The closest measure of a specific bond’s energy in a polyatomic molecule is called a bond-dissociation energy.
The energy of a chemical reaction can be calculated from bond-dissociation energies

Summary
Lewis dot diagrams are one method of explaining the observed valence of the first 20 elements
In these diagrams covalent bonding is shown as  sharing of valence electron pairs between atoms so that atoms can have close octet structures
Single, double and triple bonds have one, two and three pairs of shared electrons
In ionic bonds, electrons are transferred so the resulting ions have close octets.

Chemical Kinetics

Reversibility of Chemical Reactions: Equilibrium
Chemical reactions are reversible
Under certain conditions (concentration, temperature) both reactants and products exist together in equilibrium state

Equilibrium State
Dynamic balance: Both forward and reverse reactions continue to occur
Spontaneous: Systems move towards an equilibrium state spontaneously
Reversible: The nature and properties of the equilibrium state are the same, regardless of the direction from which it is reached

The Equilibrium Constant
aA  + bB « cC + dD

Reaction Rates
Thermodynamics determines which direction any change must take place to give equilibrium
Chemical kinetics tells us at what rate the change will take place and how the manner in which it takes place affects these rates (temperature, concentration)

Reaction Rates
Net reaction rate = forward rate – reverse rate

Reaction Rates
aA  + bB à cC + dD
Forward rate =  -1/a d[A]/dt =
-1/b d[B]/dt =
  1/c d[C]/dt =
       1/d d[D]/dt

The Integrated Rate Law
How does the concentration depend on time?

Rate Constants
The rate constant, k, is the rate of the reaction when all concentrations are 1
Each reaction is characterized by its own rate constant, depending on the nature of the reactants and the temperature
Understanding and predicting the rate constants based on the electronic structure of reactants and products is one of the goals of Theoretical Chemistry

Reaction Orders
n, m are not necessarily equal to the stoichoimetric coefficients in the net reaction
In general, the order with respect to each reagent must be found experimentally and cannot be predicted or deduced from the balanced equation for the reaction

Reaction Mechanisms and Rate Laws
Reaction mechanism: The collection of elementary processes by which an overall reaction occurs
Unimolecular process: e.g. decomposition
O3* à O2 + O
Bimolecular process: e.g.
Cl + CH4 à CH3 + HCl
Termolecular process: Extremely rare.

Elementary Processes and Rate Laws
The order of an elementary process is predictable

Reaction Mechanism and Rate Law
3A + 2B à C + D

Reaction rates and equilibria
This relationship holds for the equilibrium constant and the rate constants for the forward and reverse of any elementary process

The Principle of Detailed Balancing
The principle of microscopic reversibility:
For reaction with complex mechanisms, the condition for equilibrium is that each elementary process and its reverse proceed at the same rate

Transition States
High energy activated complexes of the reactants that must be formed if reaction is to lead to products.
Have only a transitory existence
Once formed, they must lead to products or lose excess energy and return to reactants

Stochastic pi-Calculus

Coupled chemical reactions as stochastic processes*
Xi - current number of molecules of chemical    species Si in V.
Rm (m=1,…,M) – The (elementary) chemical reactions in which the N species may participate
cm, reaction parameters. cmdt  is the average probability that a particular combination of Rm reactant molecules will react accordingly in the next time interval dt.

Reaction parameters and reaction rate constants
For a bimolecular reaction
Si+Sj à products (i¹j), with no correlations or fluctuations

Coupled chemical reactions as stochastic processes*
P (t,m) dt – The probability at time t that the next reaction in V will occur in the differential time interval (t+t,t+t+dt) and will be an Rm reaction
hmcm dt     – The probability that an Rm reaction will occur in V in the next time interval dt .
hm             - Number of distinct molecular reactant combinations for reaction Rm found in V at time t. e.g. for the bimolecular reaction above hm= Xi+Xj

Gillespie’s Algorithm for Exact Stochastic Simulation of Coupled Chemical Reactions
A Monte-Carlo technique to simulate the stochastic process described by P(t,m)
0)   Initialization:  Set t0, tstop, reaction parameters c1-cM and types, species quantities X1-XN. Calculate hici
Monte-Carlo step: Select random t and m according to P(t,m)
Update system: Advance t and update products and reactant species to reflect one Rm reaction. Re-calculate hici for reactions whose reactants have changed
Repeat 1-2 until t >= tstop

Gillespie’s Algorithm for Exact Stochastic Simulation of Coupled Chemical Reactions
Each run – one possible realization
Must carry out several independent runs from same initial conditions but with different random seeds
Number of runs depends on system (and desired confidence)
In practice, between 3 and 10 runs often suffice

Monte-Carlo Step

Stochastic (Chemical) pi-Calculus
Every channel is attached with the reaction parameter (“base rate”) of its corresponding reaction
2.  A global (external) clock is maintained
3.  The “actual rate” (cmhm)of the reaction is determined. For a bimolecular reaction:
Rate = <base rate> * <#sends> * <#receives>
4.  The clock is advanced and a communication is selected according to Gillespie’s algorithm
5.  The selected communication is carried out
6.  Steps 3-5 are repeated (until t<tstop)

Stochastic pi-Calculus
A base rate is defined for each channel when the channel is declared. A base rate is a real non-negative number
A channel with a base rate of 0 acts as a sink – all messages on the channel are discarded
If no specific base rate is determined, then the default base rate applies
If no default base rate is determined, then the default is infinite
Infinite channels and alternative rate calculation methods will be discussed later (below and Unit 3)

Using BioPSI

BioPSI architecture

Channels
Each channel is a mutable FCP record, associated with a base rate: finite (> 0) or infinite
Processes send requests to channels through FCP vector (e.g. send, receive, withdraw)
If rate inifinite: Request satisfied when enabled
If rate finite: Requests are queued

Processes
Each process is transformed to an FCP procedure
The channel set associated with each process is identified (global, arguments, newly declared, and input-bound)
Increments and decrements (via monitor) the channels’ reference counts, to monitor channel propagation and termination
